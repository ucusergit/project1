Medecine

Ibn-Sina

Widely known as "Avicenna," Ibn Sina was a remarkable polymath whose contributions spanned diverse fields, including medicine, psychology, pharmacology, geology, physics, astronomy, chemistry, and philosophy. He was also an accomplished poet, Islamic scholar, and theologian. His most significant medical work was Al-Qanun Fi Al-Tibb (The Canon of Medicine), a monumental five-volume encyclopedia containing over a million words, compiling medical knowledge from ancient and Islamic sources. Another major work, The Book of Healing, served as both a scientific and philosophical encyclopedia.

Early Life

Ibn Sina was born around 980 A.D. in the village of Afshana near Bukhara, present-day Uzbekistan, which was also his mother’s hometown. His father, Abdullah, an advocate of the Ismaili sect, was originally from Balkh, now in Afghanistan. By age 10, Ibn Sina had memorized the Quran, earning the title of Hafiz, and by 14, he had surpassed his teachers with his exceptional intellectual abilities. He pursued studies in jurisprudence, philosophy, natural science, logic, Euclid, and Ptolemy’s Almagest. Struggling initially to comprehend Aristotle's work on metaphysics, he turned to prayer and ultimately found clarity through a manual by philosopher al-Farabi.

Contributions and Achievements

By age 16, Ibn Sina focused on medicine and, by 18, became a renowned physician. His reputation soared after curing Nuh II, the ruler of the Samanids, from a severe illness that had confounded other physicians. In gratitude, the ruler granted him access to an exclusive royal library, which further enriched his studies. After his father’s death, 22-year-old Ibn Sina moved to Jurjan near the Caspian Sea, where he lectured on logic and astronomy. There, he met the famous scholar Abu Rayhan al-Biruni. Later, he traveled to Rey and Hamadan (both in present-day Iran), where he taught, wrote extensively, and cured Shams al-Dawla, the Emir of Hamadan, from a critical illness. His epic works were completed in Isfahan, though political turmoil and overexertion took a toll on his health. In his final years, he served Ala al-Dawla Muhammad, a military commander, as a physician and scientific advisor. Ibn Sina died in June 1037 A.D. at the age of 58 and was buried in Hamadan, Iran.

His Work

Ibn Sina's The Canon of Medicine was his most notable contribution to medical science. This massive encyclopedia, comprising over a million words, became a cornerstone of medical knowledge and was translated into Latin in the 12th century, serving as the standard medical text in European universities until the mid-17th century. Another seminal work, The Book of Healing, was intended to "heal" the soul and covered four areas: logic, natural sciences, mathematics, and metaphysics. In this book, he introduced "Avicennian logic" and made significant astronomical contributions, including proposing that Venus was closer to the Sun than the Earth and inventing an instrument for observing star coordinates. He also observed that stars are self-luminous and developed mathematical concepts such as the "casting out of nines." In addition to his scientific pursuits, Ibn Sina wrote poetry and contributed to music and religious studies. Out of over 400 works attributed to him, about 240 have survived, solidifying his legacy as one of history’s greatest minds.

Al-Razi

Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi (864 CE – November 19, 923 CE), known in the West as Rhazes, was a Persian-born Muslim physician, chemist, philosopher, and mathematician, as well as a leading figure of the Golden Age of Science. Sigrid Hunke, in her book The Sun of Arabs Shines on the West, described him as "the greatest physician of all time." Al-Razi authored Al-Hawi fi al-Tibb (The Comprehensive Book on Medicine), which compiled medical knowledge from Greek sources up to 925 CE and remained a key medical reference in Europe for 400 years. His expertise extended across mathematics, medicine, philosophy, astronomy, chemistry, logic, and literature.

Al-Razi earned fame in Rayy (modern-day Tehran), where he led a hospital, traveled widely, and wrote extensively on diseases. Many of his works, translated into Latin, influenced medicine until the 17th century. Among his most notable texts are The History of Medicine, Kitab al-Mansuri fi al-Tibb (The Book of Medicine Dedicated to Al-Mansur), and Kitab al-Adwiyah al-Mufradah (The Book of Simple Drugs), which features detailed anatomical descriptions. He pioneered surgical sutures, developed ointments, and advanced pharmacology through groundbreaking contributions. Over his lifetime, he authored more than 200 works on various scientific subjects.

Life and Education

Al-Razi was likely born in Rayy around 865 CE. He advocated for medical students to train in densely populated cities, where they could encounter diverse cases and learn from experienced physicians. Contrary to earlier misconceptions, evidence from an Oxford Bodleian Library manuscript, Experiments, suggests he practiced medicine in his youth, spending time in Baghdad to study.

After his studies, Al-Razi returned to Rayy to manage its hospital at the request of the local governor, Mansur ibn Ishaq, to whom he dedicated Kitab al-Mansuri fi al-Tibb and Al-Tibb al-Ruhani (Spiritual Medicine). The former addressed physical illnesses, while the latter focused on mental health. He later served as the head of Al-Mu’tadidi Hospital in Baghdad, established by Caliph Al-Mu’tadid. In his later years, Al-Razi returned to Rayy, where he went blind due to cataracts and passed away in 923 or 932 CE.

Character and Philosophy

Al-Razi’s writings reflect his humility and simplicity. In The Philosophical Biography, he wrote: "No greed for wealth, no extravagance, no disputes or injustices can be attributed to me. My conduct in food, drink, attire, and possessions remains modest and free from excess."

He emphasized the primacy of reason in his work. In Spiritual Medicine, he stated: "Reason is the supreme judge and guide; it must not be subjugated or overruled but should remain the master to whom all things are referred."

Al-Razi admired Socratic philosophy and promoted its application to science. He believed in building upon the work of predecessors, stating in Al-Mansuri: "One cannot advance in science without building on the work of earlier scholars. A single lifetime is insufficient to acquire the knowledge they amassed over centuries. Thus, those who follow their path achieve in a short time what took them lifetimes."

Contributions to Medicine and Chemistry

Medicine
  • Developed surgical sutures and ointments.
  • Provided detailed anatomical descriptions in Kitab al-Adwiyah al-Mufradah.
  • Revolutionized pharmacy and pharmacology with precise formulations and innovative methods.
  • Introduced systematic approaches to diagnosing and treating diseases.
Chemistry
  • Categorized materials into four groups: minerals, plants, animals, and derived substances.
  • Classified metals based on their properties.
  • Prepared acids, such as sulfuric acid ("oil of vitriol"), and alcohol through fermentation and distillation, techniques still used today.
  • Invented the "natural balance," a device for measuring the specific gravity of liquids.

Al-Razi’s legacy endures as one of the most influential figures in medicine, chemistry, and philosophy, with his contributions shaping scientific thought for centuries.

Ibn Al-Nafis

Ibn al-Nafis, born Ala al-Din Abu al-Hassan Ali Ibn Abi-Hazm al-Qarshi al-Dimashqi in 1213 in Damascus, was a prominent figure in medieval Islamic science. He studied medicine at the Bimaristan al-Noori in Damascus and later worked in Cairo at the Al-Nassri and Al-Mansouri hospitals, ultimately becoming the chief physician at the latter. At just 29, he published his influential Commentary on Anatomy in Avicenna's Canon, where he introduced pioneering ideas on pulmonary circulation and heart function. Although he also worked on an ambitious medical encyclopedia, The Comprehensive Book of Medicine, it was never completed. Ibn al-Nafis was a Sunni Muslim, but his intellectual pursuits spanned law, philosophy, theology, sociology, astronomy, and even literature. He is credited with writing one of the earliest Arabic novels, Theologus Autodidactus, a science-fiction tale about a child raised on an isolated island.

Pulmonary Circulation

During Ibn al-Nafis' time, the medical theories of Galen and Avicenna were widely accepted. Galen's model described blood as being formed in the liver and then passing to the right ventricle, where it was believed to travel through invisible pores in the heart's septum to the left ventricle. This theory lasted for over a millennium despite its puzzling nature. According to Galen, blood then became "vital spirit" in the left ventricle, where it mixed with air and spread throughout the body.

In contrast, Ibn al-Nafis, in his Commentary on Anatomy in Avicenna's Canon, made three key advancements:

  1. He rejected Galen's idea of porous heart septa. He asserted that the heart’s interventricular septum is solid and does not allow blood to pass through.
  2. He argued that the blood from the right ventricle must reach the left ventricle via the lungs, not through any septal pores.
  3. 3. Ibn al-Nafis predicted the existence of small connections between the pulmonary artery and vein, which was later confirmed as the pulmonary capillaries by Marcello Malpighi in the 17th century.

It took several centuries before European scholars, including Michael Servetus, recognized that blood must pass through the lungs to reach the left ventricle. Servetus was the first to describe this in 1553, and similar ideas were later echoed by Realdus Columbus, Andreas Vesalius, and William Harvey. Harvey’s experiments in the 17th century confirmed the pulmonary circulation, though he had initially struggled with the concept.

Interestingly, Ibn al-Nafis’ manuscript was rediscovered only about 80 years ago by Egyptian physician Muhyo Al-Deen el Tatawi, who found it in the Prussian State Library in Berlin. Max Meyerhof, a renowned orientalist, later translated and published parts of Ibn al-Nafis' work, bringing his contributions to light in the West.

Despite the similarities between Ibn al-Nafis' and Servetus' ideas, it's unclear whether Servetus was aware of Ibn al-Nafis' work. Although some translations of Ibn al-Nafis' writings had been made earlier, they did not include his theories on pulmonary circulation. Many historians believe Servetus independently discovered this concept, but both Ibn al-Nafis and Servetus are credited with advancing our understanding of blood circulation in the body.
