Physics

Ibn-Alhaytham

Hassan Ibn al-Haytham (commonly Latinized as Alhazen, c. 965–c. 1040) was a prominent mathematician, astronomer, and physicist of the Islamic Golden Age, hailing from what is now Iraq. Known as "the father of modern optics," he made groundbreaking contributions to the understanding of optics and visual perception. His most influential work, Kitāb al-Manāẓir (The Book of Optics), written between 1011 and 1021, significantly shaped scientific thought and was later cited by figures such as Isaac Newton, Johannes Kepler, Christiaan Huygens, and Galileo Galilei during the scientific revolution.

Ibn al-Haytham was the first to accurately explain the theory of vision, arguing that vision occurs in the brain rather than the eyes and is influenced by personal experience. He also introduced the principle of least time for refraction, later formalized as Fermat's principle. His research on reflection, refraction, and the behavior of light advanced the fields of catoptrics and dioptrics. He emphasized the importance of hypotheses being tested through experiments and mathematical reasoning, positioning him as a pioneer of the scientific method centuries before the Renaissance. Often referred to as the "world’s first true scientist," Ibn al-Haytham also contributed to philosophy, theology, and medicine. Born in Basra, Iraq, to a family of either Arab or Persian origin, Ibn al-Haytham initially studied religion before turning to mathematics and science. He served as a vizier in Basra and gained fame for his engineering expertise, including a plan to regulate the flooding of the Nile. Later, in Cairo, he held an administrative role under the Fatimid Caliph Al-Hakim but faced difficulties fulfilling his duties. According to legend, he feigned madness and lived under house arrest until Al-Hakim's death in 1021, during which time he wrote The Book of Optics. He spent the remainder of his life in Cairo, living off his writings, and died around 1040.

The Book of Optics

The Book of Optics, Ibn al-Haytham’s seven-volume treatise, was written between 1011 and 1021 and became one of his most influential works. He was the first to propose that vision occurs when light reflects off objects and enters the eyes, and he demonstrated that perception occurs in the brain. The treatise was translated into Latin in the 12th or 13th century, gaining widespread recognition during the Middle Ages and Renaissance. It was printed in 1572 under the title Opticae Thesaurus, further solidifying its impact.

Contributions to Optics and Vision

In classical antiquity, two main theories of vision prevailed: the emission theory (eyes emit light) and the intromission theory (objects send physical forms into the eye). Ibn al-Haytham synthesized ideas from earlier thinkers like Ptolemy, Euclid, and Galen to propose a more comprehensive theory. He argued that light and color travel in straight lines from objects and that the eye perceives only the perpendicular rays entering it, resolving issues of image formation. Although some aspects of his theory were later refined, his work laid the foundation for modern optics, influencing later scientists like Kepler, who built on his framework to develop the theory of retinal imaging.

Ibn al-Haytham’s contributions to the understanding of light, vision, and the scientific method were revolutionary, shaping the course of science for centuries and earning him recognition as one of the greatest minds of the Islamic Golden Age.

Al-Jazari

Badīʿ az-Zaman Abu l-ʿIzz ibn Ismāʿīl al-Jazarī (1136–1206) was a prominent Muslim polymath from Mesopotamia, known for his contributions as a scholar, inventor, mechanical engineer, artisan, and artist. He is most famous for his book The Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices (1206), which described 50 mechanical devices, including instructions for constructing them. His work laid the foundation for modern robotics, earning him the title "father of robotics." Notably, he invented the famous elephant clock and he is the father of the modern shafts used in car engines. Leonardo Devinci grow up learning for the invention of Al-jazari.

Al-Jazari was born in 1136 in Upper Mesopotamia, though his exact birthplace is uncertain. He served as the chief engineer at the Artuqid Palace, working under the Artuqid dynasty, a vassal of the Zengid and later the Ayyubid dynasty. His background is unclear, with sources variously describing him as Arab, Kurdish, or Persian. Al-Jazari's work was more focused on craftsmanship than theoretical innovation, often relying on trial and error to assemble his machines. His book was widely circulated and described devices he personally constructed, resembling a modern "do-it-yourself" guide. Al-Jazari was influenced by earlier engineers, such as the Banū Mūsā brothers and Pseudo-Archimedes, but he improved upon their designs and introduced original innovations, including mechanisms not seen before. His inventions included early examples of the crankshaft, crank-slider mechanisms, and water-raising machines, which had significant impact on modern engineering.

In 1206, the Artuqid ruler commissioned Al-Jazari to produce the first edition of his Book of Knowledge of Ingenious Mechanical Devices. Al-Jazari’s machines featured advanced mechanisms such as camshafts and crankshafts, which transformed rotary motion into linear motion—a fundamental principle in modern machinery.

He introduced several innovative construction methods, such as using wooden templates, calibrating orifices, and casting metals with sand molds. His hydropowered machines included the saqiya chain pump and the double-action suction pump, which employed the crankshaft and connecting rod mechanism, innovations vital to the development of engines. His pumps could lift water to remarkable heights, demonstrating early applications of suction pipes and reciprocating motion.

Al-Jazari also designed complex water systems and fountains, many with automata, such as a musical robot band and a drink-serving waitress. His automata were not only for entertainment but also served practical functions, such as a hand-washing device with a flush mechanism and a peacock fountain that dispensed water and offered soap. He contributed greatly to the development of water clocks, including portable, astronomical, and candle-powered clocks. His elephant clock, for instance, was a remarkable innovation that included an automaton responding at set intervals and an accurate method for tracking time throughout the year.

Al-Jazari's work in automata, clockmaking, and water-driven machinery had a lasting influence on both engineering and entertainment, making him a central figure in the history of mechanical innovation.
