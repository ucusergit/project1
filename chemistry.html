Chemistry

Jabir ibn Hayyan

Jabir ibn Hayyan, also known as Jabir al-Kufi, he is also known as the father of chemistry. He was a scholar in philosophy, natural sciences, literature, astronomy, and chemistry. He was of Yemeni Arab Azd ancestry. After the establishment of the Abbasid Caliphate, he lived in Baghdad, with his lifespan roughly spanning from 721 CE to 815 CE. Jabir is credited with founding and advancing experimental chemistry. Through experimentation and extensive reading, he made numerous discoveries in the field of chemistry, earning him titles such as "Father of Chemistry," "Chemistry of Jabir," and "Master of Chemists."

Early Life

Jabir ibn Hayyan grew up in Tus and worked alongside his father in the apothecary trade. From a young age, he was curious about natural phenomena, asking his father questions about nature, herbs, plants, their cultivation, and their benefits or harms. He also studied the properties of metals. Recognizing Jabir’s potential to become a great scholar, his father ensured he received extensive education in medicine, botany, chemistry, and ancient philosophy.

Fields of Study

Chemistry

Jabir ibn Hayyan is considered one of the earliest scholars to focus on chemistry. He is regarded as a pioneer in advancing both chemistry and experimental sciences. His name became closely associated with the experimental method due to his approach of conducting chemical experiments in his private laboratory. He didn’t rely solely on ancient chemical texts and theories but actively conducted experiments, leading to the discovery of new chemical compounds previously unknown. Jabir’s experimental method in chemistry is summarized as follows:

  • The experimenter must understand the purpose and reason behind the experiment.
  • They should be well-versed in the guidelines and instructions.
  • Avoid undertaking impossible steps.
  • Conduct experiments in a designated, isolated laboratory away from residential areas.
  • Surround themselves with trustworthy colleagues.
  • Dedicate sufficient time to perform experiments accurately.
  • Be patient and avoid being deceived by appearances, taking time to carefully analyze results
Some of his discoveries:
  • Discovered caustic soda (sodium hydroxide, NaOH).
  • Was the first to produce "aqua regia," a mixture capable of dissolving gold.
  • Introduced the method of separating gold from silver using acid solutions, a process still widely used today.
  • Was the first to discover nitric acid.
  • Was the first to discover hydrochloric acid.
  • Believed in the concept of spontaneous generation.
  • Added two essences, sulfur and mercury, to the four classical Greek elements and later Arabs added a third essence, salt.
  • Was the first to discover sulfuric acid, which he called "oil of vitriol."
  • Improved techniques for evaporation, filtration, fusion, crystallization, and distillation.
  • Prepared numerous chemical compounds, including mercuric sulfide and arsenious oxide.
  • Developed the first known distillation method in the world by inventing a distillation device. This included a glass apparatus with a long funnel, known in the West today as the "Alembic," derived from the Arabic word "Al-Inbiq." He also enhanced the quality of the glass used in this tool by adding manganese dioxide.
  • Produced fire-resistant paper.
  • Detailed the preparation of arsenic and antimony.
Astronomy

Jabir studied astronomy, defining it as the structure of the external universe. He described its substance as the elements that fill this structure. According to Jabir, the external universe consists of four fundamental elements: moisture, coldness, dryness, and heat. These elements combine to form various substances, with coldness and heat acting as active elements, while moisture and dryness are passive.

Natural Sciences

Jabir explored nature, particularly plants, animals, and stones. He classified animals into four categories: flying, crawling, swimming, and walking, noting that each category possesses moisture, coldness, essence, dryness, heat, and a spirit, all coexisting in time and space. He viewed plants as intermediate between stones and animals, sharing with animals traits like moisture, heat, essence, dryness, and coldness but lacking intellect. Major Works

Jabir ibn Hayyan authored numerous books across a variety of scientific and philosophical disciplines. Some of his most notable works include:

  • The Book of Poisons and Their Antidotes: This famous book contains five chapters discussing various types of poisons, including mineral, plant, and animal toxins, and the treatments for their effects on the human body. It bridges chemistry and medicine.
  • The Great Book of Properties: The original manuscript is preserved in the British Museum.
  • The Book of Iron: This discusses the extraction of iron from its natural sources.
  • Other works: Al-Tadbeer (The Art of Transformation), Nihayat al-Itqan (The Ultimate Perfection), Al-Mizan (The Balance), and Kitab al-Rahmah (The Book of Mercy).
